The Layered Light

Three patterns · one light

Three discs rest one atop the other on a single chain — two of polished steel, and at the centre a heart of gold.

Each is a complete sacred pattern in its own right. Layered, they tell one story: every shape is already hidden inside the one before it.

Why a shape is said to hold light

The oldest patterns we know

For thousands of years people have drawn the same handful of figures — in temple stone, in cathedral glass, in the notebooks of Leonardo da Vinci — believing that form itself can carry meaning, and even energy.

The reasoning is quietly elegant. The proportions that shape a galaxy, a nautilus shell, a snowflake and a dividing cell are not random; they repeat. A pattern that echoes the architecture of nature is thought to resonate with it — to gather and steady whatever attention is brought near.

You needn't take that literally to feel it. Whether the energy lives in the geometry or in the meaning you lend it, a symbol worn close becomes a daily, wordless reminder of how you mean to move through the world. That is the spirit in which this necklace was made.

Flower of Life

Polished steel

The seed

One circle, drawn again a single radius away — and again — until the plane fills with overlapping rings. Nothing is ever added but that same circle, yet a perfect six-fold symmetry blooms on its own. Inside it sit the Seed of Life, the lens-shaped vesica piscis, and the exact spacing of a honeycomb.

Versions of it survive on a granite wall of the Temple of Osiris at Abydos in Egypt, in Phoenician and Chinese art, and in Leonardo's studies of proportion. Many traditions read it as the blueprint of creation: endless ordered form unfolding from a single act.

It echoes the way life actually multiplies — one cell into two, two into four. Worn, it is a reminder that you belong to a larger pattern, and that all of it grows from the same first circle.

Said to carryunity, fertility, and the quiet wholeness of all things.

Metatron's Cube

Polished steel

The hidden architecture

Mark the centre of every circle in the Flower and join them with straight lines. The soft curves fall away and a rigid scaffolding steps forward — thirteen points bound into a lattice the eye reads as a cube turning in space.

Concealed in those lines are all five Platonic solids — tetrahedron, cube, octahedron, dodecahedron, icosahedron — the only five perfectly regular forms, which Plato matched to fire, earth, air, water and the heavens. The figure takes its name from Metatron, the archangel who in Jewish mystical texts stands at the threshold of creation as scribe of the divine.

Where the Flower is soft and growing, the Cube is structure and balance. It is worn as a reminder that beneath apparent chaos there is order — and as a kind of guard, drawing scattered energy back into shape.

Said to carrybalance, protection, and the order beneath the surface.

Tree of Life

Solid gold

The soul's map

The central glyph of Kabbalah, Jewish mysticism's map of how the Infinite pours into the world. Ten sefirot — Crown, Wisdom, Understanding, Mercy, Strength, Beauty, Endurance, Splendour, Foundation and Kingdom — are joined by twenty-two paths, one for each letter of the Hebrew alphabet. The Sefer Yetzirah, the ancient Book of Formation, calls them together the thirty-two paths of wisdom.

Light is said to descend the Tree from the Crown to the earthly Kingdom — and the soul climbs the same paths home. Its three columns are a teaching in themselves: mercy on the right, restraint on the left, and balance down the centre, where Beauty sits at the heart.

Cast in gold at the centre of the necklace, the Tree rests over the heart — over Tiferet, its own sphere of beauty and balance. And each of its ten points lands exactly on a centre already present in the Cube and the Flower: the map of the soul was latent in the geometry from the very first circle.

Said to carrythe path from crown to ground, and the balancing of giving with restraint.

Small contains large

Why it fractals

Slide the three discs across one another and the negative space resolves into a single mandala. This is no trick of design — the three share one hidden grid. The Flower's circle-centres become the Cube's thirteen points; ten of those same points become the Tree.

So each shape is nested in the last. The Flower hides the Cube; the Cube carries the Tree; and the Tree returns you to a single point — one circle, where it all began.

Worn close

Holding it in mind

A talisman is only as alive as the attention you bring it. Worn against the chest, let the three layers be three small reminders through the day —

the Flower — that you are part of something whole, and it all grows from one thing;

the Cube — that a steady structure holds underneath, even when the day does not;

the Tree — that the way up and the way down are one path, walked with an even hand.

Some feel an energy in the metal; some feel only the weight of the meaning. Either way it does the same quiet work — it brings you back to centre.
